 CHANGE 1: WGS-84 OBLATE SPHEROID EARTH (was: perfect sphere)
================================================================================

File: gui/earth_3d.py — create_earth(), _create_oblate_spheroid()
File: models/constants.py — new constants added

WHAT CHANGED:
  Earth is now rendered as a WGS-84 oblate spheroid instead of a perfect
  sphere. The equatorial radius (6378.137 km) is used for the mesh, and
  the Z-axis is scaled by the ratio b/a (polar/equatorial = 0.99665) to 
  reproduce the real flattening of f = 1/298.257223563.

  New constants added:
    EARTH_SEMI_MAJOR = 6378.137 km
    EARTH_SEMI_MINOR = 6356.752314245 km
    EARTH_FLATTENING = 1/298.257223563
    EARTH_ECCENTRICITY_SQ (derived)

 CHANGE 2: ATMOSPHERIC LIMB GLOW (new feature)
================================================================================

File: gui/earth_3d.py — create_earth() (new atmosphere sphere)

WHAT CHANGED:
  A semi-transparent blue sphere (~1.5% larger than Earth's equatorial radius,
  representing ~96 km altitude) is rendered around the Earth to simulate the
  atmospheric scattering glow visible from space. This is a simplified
  single-layer approximation of atmospheric Rayleigh scattering.

REFERENCES:
  [1] Nishita, T., Sirai, T., Tadamura, K., Nakamae, E. (1993)
      "Display of the Earth Taking into Account Atmospheric Scattering"
      SIGGRAPH '93 Proceedings, pp. 175-182
      ACM Digital Library: https://doi.org/10.1145/166117.166140

  [2] Real atmosphere extends to ~100 km (Kármán line).
      International Astronautical Federation definition.

================================================================================

                         _geodetic_to_cartesian_wgs84()

WHAT CHANGED:
  Ground station positions are now computed using the WGS-84 ellipsoid
  (same formula as orbit.py's _geodetic_to_ecef), replacing the previous
  spherical approximation: r * cos(lat) * cos(lon), etc.

  position vector, which is only correct for a sphere.

 CHANGE 4: PROPER GEODESIC COVERAGE FOOTPRINT (was: flat-plane approximation)
================================================================================

File: gui/earth_3d.py — add_coverage_circle()

WHAT CHANGED:
  The satellite coverage footprint is now a proper geodesic circle on the
  Earth's surface, computed using:
  
  1. Earth central angle formula:
     ψ = arccos(RE/(RE+h) · cos(El_min)) - El_min
     This gives the angular radius of the coverage area as seen from
     Earth's center.
  
  2. Rodrigues' rotation formula to generate circle points on the sphere:
     v_rot = v·cos(θ) + (k×v)·sin(θ) + k·(k·v)·(1-cos(θ))
     Points are first tilted away from the subsatellite point by ψ,
     then rotated 360° around the subsatellite axis.

  Previously, the code created a planar circle with an arbitrary 0.1 scaling
  factor that did not correspond to any physical quantity.

REFERENCES:
  [1] Roddy, D. (2006)
      "Satellite Communications", 4th Edition
      McGraw-Hill, Section 3.2: Coverage Area
      Eq. 3.2: Central angle geometry
      ISBN: 978-0071462983

  [2] Rodrigues, O. (1840)
      "Des lois géométriques qui régissent les déplacements d'un système
       solide dans l'espace"
      Journal de Mathématiques Pures et Appliquées, 5, pp. 380–440

  [3] Wertz, J. R. (2011)
      "Space Mission Engineering: The New SMAD"
      Microcosm Press, Section 5.3: Earth Coverage
      ISBN: 978-1881883159

 CHANGE 5: ECI-TO-ECEF ROTATION IN LOOK ANGLES (was: frame mixing bug)
================================================================================

File: models/orbit.py — calculate_look_angles(), new _eci_to_ecef() method,
                        calculate_ground_track() refactored

WHAT CHANGED:
  Previously, the satellite position was computed in ECI (Earth-Centered
  Inertial) coordinates but the ground station was in ECEF (Earth-Centered
  Earth-Fixed). These two frames diverge over time due to Earth's rotation,
  so subtracting them directly gave incorrect range vectors (and thus
  wrong azimuth/elevation) for any t ≠ 0.

  Fix: A new static method _eci_to_ecef() applies the rotation matrix Rz(θ_GMST)
  to convert the satellite ECI position to ECEF before computing the range
  vector. This uses:
    θ_GMST = ω_E · t  (simplified for short arcs from epoch)
    where ω_E = 7.2921159×10⁻⁵ rad/s

  The ground track computation was also refactored to use this same
  _eci_to_ecef() method for consistency (previously it applied the rotation
  inline with slightly different logic).

REFERENCES:
  [1] Vallado, D. A. (2013), Eq. 3-82
      "Fundamentals of Astrodynamics and Applications"
      IAU-76/FK5 GMST approximation

  [2] Vallado (2013), Algorithm 28 — RAZEL (Range, Azimuth, Elevation)
      Section 4.4.2 — Topocentric SEZ frame

  [3] IERS Conventions 2010 (IERS Technical Note No. 36)
      Chapter 5: Transformation between the ITRS and GCRS
      https://www.iers.org/IERS/EN/Publications/TechnicalNotes/tn36.html

 CHANGE 6: J2 OBLATENESS PERTURBATION (new feature)
================================================================================

File: models/orbit.py — _compute_j2_rates(), get_satellite_position_eci()

WHAT CHANGED:
  The orbit propagator now includes secular (long-term average) perturbations
  due to Earth's J2 zonal harmonic (oblateness). Three orbital elements are
  affected:

  1. RAAN regression:
     Ω̇ = -1.5 · n · J2 · (RE/p)² · cos(i)
     For LEO at 53°: ~-5°/day; for GEO at 0°: ~-4.9°/century

  2. Apsidal rotation (argument of perigee precession):
     ω̇ = 1.5 · n · J2 · (RE/p)² · (2 - 2.5·sin²(i))
     At critical inclination i=63.4° (Molniya): ω̇ = 0 (frozen perigee)

  3. Mean motion correction:
     Corrects the mean anomaly rate for J2's effect on orbital energy

  The new constant EARTH_J2 = 1.08263×10⁻³ was added to constants.py.

REFERENCES:
  [1] Vallado, D. A. (2013), Eq. 9-41 through 9-43
      "Fundamentals of Astrodynamics and Applications"
      Chapter 9: General Perturbation Techniques

  [2] Montenbruck, O. and Gill, E. (2000)
      "Satellite Orbits: Models, Methods, and Applications"
      Springer, Section 3.4: Earth Oblateness (J2)
      Eq. 3.30–3.32
      ISBN: 978-3540672807

  [3] EGM-96 / WGS-84 gravity model
      J2 value from Lemoine, F. G., et al. (1998)
      "The Development of the Joint NASA GSFC and NIMA Geopotential Model
       EGM96", NASA/TP-1998-206861

 CHANGE 7: ATMOSPHERIC DRAG MODEL FOR LEO (new feature)
================================================================================

File: models/orbit.py — _get_atmospheric_density(), _compute_drag_perturbation()
File: models/constants.py — ATMOSPHERIC_DENSITY_MODEL dict

WHAT CHANGED:
  An exponential atmospheric density model is now available for computing
  drag effects on LEO satellites. The model uses altitude-dependent
  reference density (ρ₀), reference height (h₀), and scale height (H):
    ρ(h) = ρ₀ · exp(-(h - h₀) / H)

  Drag parameters can be specified per satellite:
    - drag_area_m2: cross-sectional area (default 1.0 m²)
    - drag_coeff: aerodynamic drag coefficient (default 2.2)
    - mass_kg: satellite mass (default 100 kg)

  The LEO preset now includes Starlink-representative drag parameters:
    A = 10 m², Cd = 2.2, m = 260 kg

  Note: The drag perturbation is computed but the semi-major axis is NOT
  decremented during propagation (would require numerical integration).
  The infrastructure is in place for future enhancement.

REFERENCES:
  [1] US Standard Atmosphere 1976
      NOAA/NASA/USAF, NOAA-S/T 76-1562
      https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/19770009539

  [2] CIRA-72 (COSPAR International Reference Atmosphere 1972)
      Used for scale height values at different altitudes

  [3] Vallado, D. A. (2013), Table 8-4
      Exponential atmosphere density model

  [4] Wertz, J. R. (2011)
      "Space Mission Engineering: The New SMAD"
      Table 8-4: Typical spacecraft drag parameters

  [5] SpaceX FCC Filing (2019)
      Starlink satellite mass ~260 kg, flat-panel ~10 m² cross-section

 CHANGE 8: CORRECTED MOLNIYA (HEO) SEMI-MAJOR AXIS
================================================================================

File: models/orbit.py — create_satellite_from_type() HEO case

WHAT CHANGED:
  Previously: semi_major_axis = EARTH_RADIUS + 26550 = 32921 km
  This was incorrect — it treated 26550 km as an "altitude" but Molniya's
  average altitude is much lower than 26550 km.

  Corrected using orbital mechanics:
    Perigee altitude ≈ 500 km  → r_p = 6871 km
    Apogee altitude ≈ 39873 km → r_a = 46244 km
    Semi-major axis a = (r_p + r_a) / 2 = 26557.5 km
    Eccentricity e = (r_a - r_p) / (r_a + r_p) ≈ 0.74

  The semi-major axis is measured from Earth's center, not from the surface.

REFERENCES:
  [1] Sidi, M. J. (1997)
      "Spacecraft Dynamics and Control"
      Cambridge University Press, Chapter 4: Orbital Mechanics
      ISBN: 978-0521550727

  [2] Kidder, S. Q. and Vonder Haar, T. H. (1990)
      "On the Use of Satellites in Molniya Orbits for Meteorological
       Observation of Middle and High Latitudes"
      Journal of Atmospheric and Oceanic Technology, 7(3), pp. 517-522

  [3] Critical inclination i = 63.4° — at this inclination, J2 perturbation
      causes zero apsidal rotation (ω̇ = 0), keeping the apogee fixed over
      the northern hemisphere.
      Source: Vallado (2013) Eq. 9-42, setting ω̇ = 0:
        2 - 2.5·sin²(i) = 0  →  sin²(i) = 0.8  →  i = 63.435°

 CHANGE 9: SATELLITE VELOCITY COMPUTATION (new method)
================================================================================

File: models/orbit.py — get_satellite_velocity_eci()

WHAT CHANGED:
  New method to compute satellite velocity in ECI coordinates for any time,
  using the specific angular momentum h = √(μ·p) and perifocal velocity
  components:
    ẋ_PQW = -(μ/h)·sin(ν)
    ẏ_PQW = (μ/h)·(e + cos(ν))

  This enables proper velocity-dependent calculations (e.g., drag, Doppler
  via velocity projection) in future updates.

REFERENCES:
  [1] Vallado, D. A. (2013), Eq. 2-24, 2-36, 2-82
      "Fundamentals of Astrodynamics and Applications"
      Velocity in perifocal frame and PQW-to-IJK transformation
